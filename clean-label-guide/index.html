What Is a "Clean Label" Supplement?

A clean label supplement contains only the active ingredients and the minimum necessary excipients to hold a capsule together or prevent caking. It avoids artificial colors, sweeteners, and synthetic fillers that serve the manufacturer — not you.

The supplement industry is largely unregulated compared to pharmaceuticals. Brands can use cheap filler ingredients, hide dosages behind "proprietary blends," and add artificial dyes that serve zero health purpose. Learning to read labels is the most important skill you can develop as a supplement buyer.

The Biggest Red Flags by Severity

🔴 FD&C Artificial Dyes

Red 40, Blue 1, Yellow 5/6 — synthetic petroleum-derived dyes. Zero health benefit. Linked to hyperactivity in children.

🔴 Titanium Dioxide

Whitening agent classified as possibly carcinogenic. Banned in food in EU. Still common in US supplement coatings.

🔴 Proprietary Blend

Hides individual ingredient dosages. You can't verify if you're getting an effective dose of anything.

🔴 Hydrogenated Oils

Trans fats used as flow agents. No place in a health product. Associated with cardiovascular risk.

🟡 Sucralose

Artificial sweetener. Some research links it to gut microbiome disruption at high doses.

🟡 Acesulfame Potassium

Artificial sweetener (Ace-K). Animal studies raised concerns. Unnecessary in supplement form.

🟡 Carrageenan

Seaweed-derived thickener. Linked to GI inflammation in some individuals. Common in capsule shells.

🟡 Silicon Dioxide (high dose)

Anti-caking agent. Safe in small amounts. Concerning when listed early = large quantity.

How to Read a Supplement Label Like a Pro

1. Look at the "Other Ingredients" list

Active ingredients are in the supplement facts panel. "Other ingredients" are the additives — capsule material, flow agents, fillers, colors, sweeteners. This is where red flags hide.

2. Check ingredient order

Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. If a filler like silicon dioxide or magnesium stearate appears near the top, there's a lot of it.

3. Verify third-party certification

Certifications that actually mean something: NSF Certified for Sport, USP Verified, Informed Sport. These require independent lab testing — not just manufacturer claims.

4. Reject proprietary blends

If you see "Proprietary Blend — 500mg" with a list of 6 ingredients under it, you have no idea if you're getting 450mg of the cheapest ingredient and 10mg each of everything else. Always prefer fully disclosed dosages.
