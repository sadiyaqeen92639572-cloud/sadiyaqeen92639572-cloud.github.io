What Is Price Per Unit — And Why It Tricks You

Retailers have known for decades that the human brain is bad at comparing value when sizes are different. A 20 oz bottle of shampoo for $3.49 versus a 28 oz bottle for $4.79 — most shoppers just see "$3.49 is cheaper." It's not. The 28 oz bottle costs $0.171/oz versus $0.175/oz — you'd save money buying the bigger one.

This is why price-per-unit (also called unit price) is the single most useful number in grocery shopping. It strips away the packaging and tells you the real cost of what you're getting.

How to Calculate Price Per Unit (Formula)

The formula is simple:

Unit Price = Total Price ÷ Total Quantity
Example: $4.79 ÷ 28 oz = $0.171 per oz

The tricky part is when two products use different units — one listed per oz, another per lb. You need to convert to the same base before comparing. Our calculator handles this automatically.

When Bulk Is NOT Cheaper

Bigger isn't always better value. Here are three situations where the smaller size wins:

  • Sale prices — a sale on the small size can beat the everyday bulk price
  • Store brands vs. name brand bulk — Great Value 32 oz often beats Tide 100 oz per oz
  • Products with expiry — if you can't use it before it expires, the "cheaper" bulk option wastes money
  • Club store comparison — Costco bulk items are sometimes priced higher per unit than Walmart sale prices
